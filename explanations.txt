MOVE ORDERING: KILLER MOVES AND THE HISTORY HEURISTIC
=======================================================

Background: why move ordering matters at all
----------------------------------------------
Alpha-beta pruning only prunes well if the best move at a node tends to get
searched first. If the first move searched is already close to the true
best move, alpha (or beta, in negamax terms) tightens immediately and every
subsequent sibling can be refuted quickly or skipped outright. If the best
move is searched last, alpha-beta degrades toward examining almost the full
tree, the same as plain minimax. In the best case (perfect ordering),
alpha-beta cuts the effective branching factor from ~35 down to roughly its
square root, ~6; that's the difference between a depth-6 search finishing
in a fraction of a second and one that doesn't finish in a reasonable
amount of time at all.

The engine already has two ordering signals:
  1. The TT best-move hint (whatever move proved best the last time this
     exact position was searched, at any depth).
  2. MVV-LVA (Most Valuable Victim, Least Valuable Attacker) for captures —
     search "queen takes pawn" after "pawn takes queen", not before.

Both are strong, but neither says anything about QUIET (non-capturing)
moves. Right now all quiet moves are dumped at the back of the list in
whatever order the move generator happened to produce them, with no
information to tell them apart. Killer moves and the history heuristic are
the two standard, cheap techniques for ranking quiet moves too.


Killer moves
------------
Observation: if a quiet move caused a beta cutoff (a "refutation") at a
given PLY, a sibling node at that *same ply*, in a *different* branch of
the tree, quite often has a very similar position — same side to move,
similar material, similar threats — because it's just a different way the
opponent could have played two moves ago. So a move that just refuted one
line at ply P is a disproportionately good guess for refuting a sibling
line at ply P too, even though it's a totally different position.

Implementation: keep a small fixed-size table, indexed by ply, of the last
one or two quiet moves that caused a beta cutoff at that ply —
killers[ply][0] and killers[ply][1] in this engine. Whenever a quiet move
causes a cutoff, store it as killers[ply][0], shifting the previous
killers[ply][0] into killers[ply][1] (so the table remembers the two most
recent killers per ply, not just the single most recent). Only quiet
moves are stored — captures already have MVV-LVA, which is a strictly
better signal for them, and mixing the two would just add noise.

At move-ordering time, a move that matches killers[ply][0] or
killers[ply][1] gets a large ordering bonus — below the TT move and actual
captures, but above every other quiet move. This costs O(1) table lookups
per move and a few words of memory per ply; no search-tree information is
discarded to make room for it.

Why "at that ply" and not "in that subtree": killer moves are deliberately
NOT position-specific (unlike the TT, which is keyed on the exact zobrist
hash). They're a ply-indexed heuristic that says "moves like this tend to
work well two plies from now regardless of the exact position," which is a
much weaker but much cheaper claim than the TT's "this exact position had
this exact best move."


The history heuristic
----------------------
Killer moves only remember the last couple of cutoff moves per ply, and
they're forgotten as soon as they're overwritten. The history heuristic
takes the same underlying idea — "a move that has caused cutoffs before is
likely to be good again" — and generalizes it across the WHOLE search,
not just one ply, and with a graded score instead of a slot that gets
evicted.

Implementation: keep a table history[color][from_square][to_square]
(or [piece][to_square]; from/to is simpler and what this engine already
has on hand). Whenever a quiet move causes a beta cutoff at depth D, add
D*D (or some other function that grows with depth) to that move's history
score. Deeper cutoffs get a bigger bonus, because a cutoff found deep in
the tree represents a conclusion that survived a lot more scrutiny than one
found one ply from a leaf — it's a stronger signal.

At move-ordering time, a quiet move that isn't the TT move or a killer is
ranked by its history score instead of being left at a flat, undifferentiated
score. Over the course of a search, moves like "develop the knight to a
central square" or "castle" accumulate high scores because they're
frequently part of a refutation across many different branches, while
moves like "push the rook pawn one square" stay near zero.

Caveat — history scores need periodic decay or normalization (commonly:
halve the whole table between root searches, or use depth*depth accumulation
which naturally keeps well-behaved bounds within one search). Without
decay, scores accumulated from an early, easy position can wrongly
dominate move ordering in a much later, unrelated position reached in the
same game. This engine ages the table by halving it once per
iterative-deepening call (i.e. once per "go"), which is simple and avoids
unbounded growth across a whole game without needing per-move bookkeeping.


Where these fit relative to what's already there
--------------------------------------------------
Full move ordering priority, highest to lowest:
  1. TT best move (from a previous search of this exact position)
  2. Captures/promotions, ranked by MVV-LVA
  3. Killer moves for this ply (up to two)
  4. Remaining quiet moves, ranked by history score

Captures are deliberately ranked above killers/history: MVV-LVA is a
same-position, material-grounded signal, while killers/history are
cross-position heuristics. A capture that wins material is essentially
always worth trying before a quiet heuristic guess, no matter how good
that guess has been elsewhere.

Both techniques are applied only in the main negamax search, not in
quiescence — quiescence only ever considers captures (plus check evasions),
so there are no "quiet moves" for killers/history to help order there.
